## 1. SF Rent Control: Does It Apply to Mission Bay Condos?

**No. Mission Bay condos are exempt from SF rent control on two independent grounds.**

### Ground 1: Post-1979 Construction
SF's Rent Ordinance only covers buildings that received a certificate of final occupancy **on or before June 13, 1979**. Mission Bay is entirely post-2000 construction, so it is categorically exempt.

### Ground 2: Condominium Status (Costa-Hawkins)
Even if Mission Bay were pre-1979, the **Costa-Hawkins Rental Housing Act (1995)** exempts all **condominiums** and **single-family homes** from local rent control, regardless of construction date. This is a separate, independent exemption.

**Bottom line:** Mission Bay condos face zero local rent control restrictions. Landlords can raise rent by any amount upon lease renewal or between tenants.

### What About AB 1482 (Statewide Tenant Protection Act)?
AB 1482 caps annual rent increases at **5% + local CPI (or 10% max, whichever is lower)** for most rental housing. However, condos are **exempt** if:
1. The owner is a **natural person** (not a corporation, REIT, or LLC with a corporate member), AND
2. The owner provides the required **written statutory notice** to the tenant in the lease.

If a Mission Bay condo is owned by an individual landlord who provides proper notice, AB 1482 does **not** apply. If owned by an LLC/corporation or if notice is missing, AB 1482's 5%+CPI cap **does** apply.

**Current AB 1482 cap (Aug 2025 - Jul 2026): 6.3%** (5% + 1.3% CPI).

AB 1482 expires January 1, 2030 unless extended.

## 2. Costa-Hawkins Act: Detailed Mechanics

The Costa-Hawkins Rental Housing Act (California Civil Code sections 1954.50-1954.535) does three things:

1. **Exempts condos and SFHs** from all local rent control, regardless of age
2. **Exempts new construction** (post-Feb 1, 1995 statewide; post-1979 for SF specifically) from local rent control
3. **Guarantees vacancy decontrol** - landlords can reset rent to market rate when a tenant leaves

### Recent Repeal Attempts (All Failed)
| Measure | Year | Result |
|---------|------|--------|
| Prop 10 | 2018 | Defeated |
| Prop 21 | 2020 | Defeated |
| Prop 33 | 2024 | Defeated (62% No) |
| SB 466 | 2024 | Died in committee |

SF's Board of Supervisors passed an ordinance in Oct 2024 to extend rent control to pre-1994 buildings, but it was contingent on Prop 33 passing. Since Prop 33 failed, the 1979 cutoff remains.

**Risk assessment:** Costa-Hawkins repeal has failed three times at the ballot box by wide margins. Legislative repeal remains possible but politically unlikely in the near term.

## 3. Historical SF Rent Data & Growth Rates

### The COVID Crash (Hard Evidence That Rents Can Decline Dramatically)

| Period | Metric | Change |
|--------|--------|--------|
| Jan-Dec 2020 | Median 1BR (Zumper) | $3,500 -> $2,700 (-22.6%) |
| 2020 overall | Average rents (RentHub) | -27% |
| Feb 2020 - Apr 2021 | Median 2BR | -24% |
| Peak-to-trough | Weighted avg asking rent | -23% below 2015 peak |

Luxury segment vacancy rates hit **~20%** in 2020 (vs. ~8.5% in 2025).

### Recovery Timeline
| Year | Median 1BR | Trend |
|------|-----------|-------|
| Pre-COVID (2019) | ~$3,500-$3,700 | All-time highs |
| 2020 (Dec) | ~$2,700 | Historic crash |
| 2021 (Apr) | ~$2,600 | Rock bottom |
| 2022 | ~$2,800-$3,000 | Gradual rebound |
| 2023-2024 | ~$2,900-$3,000 | Stagnation |
| 2025 (Aug) | ~$3,400-$3,500 | Near full recovery |

**Critical finding:** It took SF rents **5+ years** to recover to pre-pandemic levels. As of mid-2025, 2BR rents just barely surpassed 2019 peaks. Over a 5-year window (2020-2025), cumulative rent growth was approximately **0%** -- all the growth was just recovering lost ground.

### Long-Term Growth Rates

| Period | Annual Real Growth (Inflation-Adjusted) | Notes |
|--------|----------------------------------------|-------|
| 2000-2010 | ~0.6%/yr | Dot-com bust, then financial crisis |
| 2010-2019 | ~2.4%/yr | Strong tech boom |
| 2019-2025 | ~0%/yr (nominal) | COVID crash + recovery |
| 5-yr (2020-2025) | ~0% nominal | Crash and recovery |

**Zillow's perspective:** SF rents rose only **4% total** from pre-pandemic to mid-2025, while LA rose 23% and NYC rose 29% over the same period.

### Is 3%/yr Rent Growth Reasonable?

**It is aggressive for SF based on recent history.** The best sustained period (2012-2019) saw ~2.4%/yr real growth. Including the 2000s and COVID era, long-run average is closer to **1-2%/yr nominal**. A 3% assumption would require sustained economic boom conditions with no recessions or shocks.

For a conservative model: **2% nominal** is more defensible.
For a moderate model: **2.5% nominal** is reasonable.
For an optimistic model: **3% nominal** is at the upper bound and assumes no crashes.

## 4. Practical Rent Growth Ceiling for $8,500/mo Units

Even without rent control, several forces constrain rent growth at the high end:

### Income Constraint
At 30% of gross income, an $8,500/mo rent requires **~$340,000/yr household income**. This limits the tenant pool to:
- Senior software engineers / engineering managers at FAANG/AI companies
- Dual-income tech couples
- Finance professionals
- Medical specialists

SF median household income is ~$136,000 (2024). The $8,500 unit targets roughly the **top 5-10%** of households.

### Competition from New Supply
Mission Bay and SoMa have seen significant luxury construction. High-end units compete with each other, creating a ceiling. When vacancy rises (as in 2020), landlords must offer concessions.

### Remote Work Structural Shift
The 2020 exodus demonstrated that high-end tenants are the most mobile. Tech workers can relocate to lower-cost areas. While RTO mandates have reversed some of this, hybrid work remains prevalent, reducing the captive nature of the SF tenant pool.

### Historical Precedent for Luxury Declines
- **2001-2003 (dot-com bust):** Luxury rents declined significantly
- **2008-2010 (financial crisis):** Moderate decline
- **2020-2021 (COVID):** Luxury segment saw the steepest declines (vacancy ~20%)

### Mission Bay Specific Outlook (2025-2026)
- Rental rates up **5% YoY** as of mid-2025
- Analysts forecast **4-6% annual growth** near-term, driven by AI hiring
- Office vacancy in Mission Bay: **12.2%** (vs 34.2% citywide) -- a strong positive signal
- Condo median price: $1,168,000 (Aug 2025), up from $900,000 trough (Jul 2024)

### Practical Ceiling Assessment
For an $8,500/mo unit, sustainable long-term rent growth is constrained by:
1. **Income growth of the top ~10%:** Historically 3-5%/yr nominal in tech boom years, but volatile
2. **Supply competition:** Mission Bay months of supply at 2.4 months (tight but not extreme)
3. **Recession/shock risk:** Every 7-10 years, rents can drop 15-25% in SF luxury
4. **Price anchoring:** Tenants at $8,500 have alternatives (buy, relocate, downsize)

**Net assessment:** 3%/yr average over 10+ years is optimistic but not impossible if no major downturn occurs. A more realistic blended rate accounting for periodic declines is **1.5-2.5%/yr**.
